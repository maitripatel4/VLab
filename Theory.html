Why do we need to measure hardness of water?

   Water contains many dissolved minerals and salts. Some contributes in a positive manner to biological animals while some in a negative manner. Some even harms our body cells and gets accumulated into our body in form of stone which needs to be operated upon and removed! According to one report, hard water causes skin irritation when it comes into direct contact of human body. Hardwater is safe to drink , but using it over a long time can lead to problems like

  • Strains in skin
  • Water appliances work harder, resulting in higher water bills
  • Spots appear on clothes and linens

    It also causes damage to hair. According to report of 2016 which was been generated based on the experiment conducted on 15 women who were asked to use hard and soft water for their hair wash for 30 days, found out that hard water decreased their hair thickness and hindered their hair growth along with ruffled appearance.
  Some additional symptoms noticed were:

  • Breakage
  • Thinning
  • Tangles
  • Dry Scalp
  • Dullness
  • Brassiness or a Green Hue
  • Frizzness

   It not only damages human body, it also causes rupture to machine parts on which it is being used for cooling or for any other production purpose. It forms solid deposits comprised of mainly calcium and magnesium salts and can damage equipments, while soft water may be corrosive and therefore it is important measuring and knowing levels of hardness in your process water in order to maintain balance between corrosivity and scaling.

What is Hardness of water?

   Hardness of Water is due to presence of soluble bicarbonates, chlorides and sulphates of calcium and magnesium. Water which doesn't give lather with soap is hard water. It has high mineral contents. As it doens't produce lather with soap, it is not suitable for laundry purpose.
There are two types of Hardness:

  • Temporary Hardness: Presence of magnesium and calcium carbonates make water temporarily hard.
  • Permanent Hardness: Presence of soluble salts in form of chlorides and sulphates in water make it hard.

The degree of hardness of drinking water has been classified in terms of equivalent CaCO3 concentration as follows:

Type of Water Hardness
Soft 0-60mg/L
Medium 60-1200mg/L
Hard 120-180mg/L
Very Hard >180mg/L

Units of Hardness

  1. Parts Per Million(ppm): Is the parts of calcium carbonate equivalent hardness per 106 parts of water,
    i.e, 1 ppm = 1 part of CaCO3 equivalent to hardness in 106 parts of water.
  2. Miligram per litre: Is the number of milligrams of CaCO3 equivalent hardness present per litre of water. Thus:
    1mg/L = 1 mg of CaCO3 equivalent hardness per gallons of water
  3. Clark's Degree(oCl): Is number of grains (1/7000 lb) of CaCO3 equivalent hardness per gallon (10 lb)of water. It is parts of CaCO 3equivalent hardness per 70,000 parts of water. Thus,
    1oClarke = 1 grain of CaCO3 equivalent hardness per gallon of water.
  4. Degree French(oFr): Is the parts of CaCO3 equivalent hardness per 105 parts of water. Thus,
    1o Fr = 1 part of CaCO3 hardness eq per 105 parts of water.

Relation Between Units of Degreee of Hardness

  • 1ppm = 1mg/L = 0.1oFr = 0.07oCl
  • 1mg/L = 1ppm = 0.1oFr = 0.07oCl
  • 1oCl = 1.43oFr = 14.3ppm = 0.7mg/L
  • 1oFr = 10ppm = 10 mg/L = 0.7oCl

Alkalinity

   Alkalinity is an aggregate property of the water sample which measures acid neutralizing capacity of water. It can be interpreted in terms specific substances only when a complete chemical composition of the sample is also performed. The alkalinity of surface water is due to the carbonate, bicarbonate and hydroxide content and is often interpreted in terms of the concentrations of these constituents.
Higher the alkalinity, greater is the capacity of water to neutralize acids. Conversely, the lower the alkalinity, the lesser will be the neutralizing capacity.
